The History of Lavender

Plant

Lavender is indigenous to the mountainous areas of the countries bordering the western European part of the Mediterranean region. When early travelers brought it back, the plant spread fairly rapidly to other parts of the world, and by the sixteenth-century lavender was already a much-loved plant in English gardens. The first lavender plants found in America made their way here sometime after the first European settlers.

Records show lavender has been in use for over 2,500 years. The early uses of lavender were at least as numerous as today's, but they tended to be more of a medicinal nature. Biblical references to lavender are found in the gospel of Luke by the name used at that time, spikenard. Lavender was also used in ancient Egypt for mummification, and the Romans scented their public bathhouses with it. The name lavender is derived from the Latin verb lavare —to wash.

Lavender's association with washing and bathing has an interesting history. In Medieval and Renaissance Europe, washerwomen were known as "lavenders" because they spread their laundry over lavender bushes to dry for the scent it gave.
Current medicinal uses promote lavender as a mild sedative and an aid to relieve neuralgia pain when used in the bath. It's also used for treating bruises and insect bites with a compress. Lavender sprays are also purported to discourage mosquitoes.

Scent

Lavender is one of the oldest perfumes used in England and in the 1500s, Queen Elizabeth I used it both as a perfume and in her tea to treat migraines. By the time of The Great Plague in 1665, it was even thought to help protect people from becoming infected and to cure those with it.

It’s known that the Egyptians made perfumes with lavender and when Tutankhamun’s tomb was opened, traces of lavender were found and its scent could still be detected.Over the coming centuries, the use of perfume became increasingly popular and so lavender has remained favoured for its fragrance, as well as its natural healing properties, ever since..

In the modern age, lavender has become a popular choice for aromatherapy, a form of therapy that uses scents to improve one’s mood.Lavender also has dozens of species and hybrids. This has caused the blooming of varieties in essential oils that may greatly or slightly differ in particular features.But, the distinctive sweet aroma of lavandula angustifolia is the one popularly featured in the essential oils available in the market.

Color

Hue

Lavender's hue sits between violet and blue on the color wheel, often described as a soft, pale purple with a cool undertone. It gets its name from the lavender flower, which naturally carries this gentle, calming shade. Depending on how much blue or red is mixed in, lavender can lean towards a cooler, bluish tint or a warmer, pinkish one. It’s often associated with tranquility, grace, and elegance, making it popular in design and fashion when a subtle, soothing vibe is needed.

Saturation

The saturation of lavender refers to how vivid or muted the color appears. Lavender typically has low to medium saturation, giving it that soft, delicate quality it’s known for. When the saturation is lower, lavender looks more muted and pastel, almost ethereal—perfect for creating calming, serene environments. Higher saturation, on the other hand, makes the color more vibrant and intense, shifting it closer to a bright lilac or even a light purple, which can feel more energetic and playful. But in most uses, lavender keeps that gentle, understated presence, which is part of its charm.

Brightness

The brightness of lavender refers to how light or dark the color appears. Lavender generally has high brightness, giving it a soft, airy, and almost luminous quality. This lightness is what makes lavender feel so calming and fresh, often evoking a sense of openness and tranquility. When you decrease the brightness, lavender shifts toward deeper purples or mauves, creating a moodier, more dramatic effect. Increase the brightness, and it moves closer to a very pale, almost white tint, often used in minimalist or delicate designs. Its naturally bright character is part of why lavender feels so light and soothing in both fashion and decor.
