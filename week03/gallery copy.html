A lavender plant is a small, bushy shrub that typically grows 1 to 3 feet tall and wide, depending on the variety. It has thin, woody stems at the base that become softer and more flexible toward the top. The stems are topped with flower spikes that can reach up to a foot in length.

The leaves are narrow, elongated, and lance-shaped, measuring about 1 to 2 inches long. They have a gray-green to silver-green hue and are often covered with fine, soft hairs, giving them a slightly fuzzy texture.

The flowers are small, tubular, and arranged in tight clusters along the tops of the stems. They range in color from pale lavender to deep violet, though some varieties may appear more blue or even pinkish. The flowers have a distinct, sweet fragrance that’s both floral and herbaceous.

The overall shape of the plant is rounded and compact, with a slightly wild, airy appearance due to the long, slender flower stems that sway in the breeze. The combination of silvery foliage and vibrant purple flowers gives lavender its iconic, delicate look.

The color lavender is a soft, pale shade of purple with a cool, calming undertone. It sits between violet and light blue on the color spectrum, often described as a mix of light purple with a touch of gray or blue to soften its appearance. Physically, it has a delicate, almost airy quality due to its lightness and subtle saturation.

Lavender can range from a very pale, almost pastel hue to a slightly deeper, richer tone, depending on the balance of blue and red in the mix. When more blue is present, it leans cooler, giving it a crisp, fresh feel. When it has a bit more red or pink, it feels warmer and softer.
The color is often associated with softness and lightness, appearing almost ethereal in certain lighting. It reflects light well, giving it a gentle, luminous quality that makes it feel soothing and tranquil. Lavender’s muted tone makes it versatile, working well in both calming, minimalist palettes and more vibrant, playful designs when paired with contrasting colors.
